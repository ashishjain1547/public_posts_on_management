 It's a bloodbath in big tech and you can blame artificial intelligence for it. Layoffs across the tech industry have accelerated this year. Companies are no longer just cutting costs. They are rewriting how work gets done. Instead of hiring more people, they're building linear, leaner teams, pardon me, where AI tools do more with fewer humans. More than 73,000 jobs have been wiped out in the first quarter alone across 95 companies, and the job cuts are far from over. While each company has its own reasons from cost cutting to restructuring, there is one common thread, and that is a deeper, more aggressive pivot towards AI. Let's start with the latest. Meta is preparing for a fresh wave of layoffs, with the first round expected around the 20th of May. The company, which owns Facebook and Instagram, plans to cut about 10 percent of its global workforce. That's close to 8000 employees in just the first phase. And more cuts could follow later this year. Reports suggest executives may even adjust the scale based on how fast AI capabilities evolve. If and when this goes through, it would be Meta's biggest round of layoffs since its 2022-2023 restructuring. That is when it cut about 21,000 jobs during what it called the year of efficiency. But this time, the company is financially stronger. So it's not about survival. CEO Mark Zuckerberg is investing heavily in AI. He's building systems that can write code and execute complex tasks autonomously. And Meta is not alone. Across big tech, this is the story. Technology company Snap Inc. is cutting around 1000 jobs. That's nearly 16 percent of its workforce. CEO Evan Spiegel says AI is helping automate repetitive tasks and speed up operations. The company is also eliminating hundreds of unfilled roles, signaling a hiring freeze. Amazon has already trimmed about 30,000 corporate jobs as it streamlines operations and doubles down on AI. Oracle, founded by Larry Ellison, has cut between 20,000 to 30,000 jobs as it pours investment into AI infrastructure. Singapore-based Crypto.com has laid off 12 percent of its staff, with CEO Chris Marsalik saying these were roles that he says, and I'm quoting here, do not adapt in our new world. Software company Adelaide has cut about 1,600 jobs, around 10 percent of its workforce, to self-fund AI investments. Co-founder Mike Cannonbrook says, and I'm quoting again, people and AI together will drive the best outcomes. Fintech company Block Inc. has also slashed jobs, citing efficiency gains from AI. And the list goes on. From Salesforce to Pinterest to WiseTech Global, companies are cutting jobs with an aim to redirect the money into AI-driven roles. Now this isn't just a wave of layoffs that we're talking about. We're talking about people, people with degrees, with student loans, home loans. They're all being let go. And that constant threat of job loss. And even if you escape the job cut, you're left with survivor's guilt. It is the stress felt by employees who keep their jobs. Can you imagine? But watch colleagues around them lose their jobs. That is what's causing survivor's guilt. The fear of being next. The pressure to do more with less. The constant uncertainty. It's now taking a serious toll on mental health. Take a look at the numbers on your screens right now. Employee health platforms are reporting a four-fold spike in telehealth consultations for mental health issues. 77% of these cases are from employees aged 21 to 30. Studies also show up to 80% of IT professionals report feeling stressed. And over 70% show symptoms of conditions like depression, insomnia and anxiety. The shift to AI isn't just changing how companies operate. It's changing how employees see themselves. Many now feel their skills are becoming obsolete. And who is most at risk? Young professionals. Early career employees are facing a loss of confidence and rising anxiety. In an increasingly competitive job market that is. A 2026 study found 44% of Gen Z employees admit that they've actively sabotaged instead of embracing the AI initiatives taken by their companies out of fear of losing their jobs. This is the new reality of Big Tech. Leaner teams, smarter systems and harder questions. Because in the end, the future of work will not just be determined by what AI can do.
