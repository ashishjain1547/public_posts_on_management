 Layoffs are no longer breaking news. They have become the background noise. Lately, it feels almost impossible to go a week without seeing a headline on company-wide layoffs. And the culprit most point to artificial intelligence. Cognizant is the latest. It is a US-based IT company. It is reportedly planning to lay off around 15,000 jobs globally. Majority of the cuts are expected to happen in India. Then there is Coinbase, the American crypto exchange. It is slashing 14% of its workforce. Payment solutions firm PayPal, that also plans to cut 20% of its staff. So far this year, more than 90,000 tech employees have been handed pink slips. A recent Gartner survey found that 80% of the companies here using AI are actively cutting staff. So artificial intelligence is not assisting jobs. It is redesigning them and in many cases, eliminating them. But this is not a layoff story. Tonight, I want to talk about how to survive a layoff. What happens if you get the pink slip? Let's be honest. A layoff doesn't just take away your salary. It takes away your routine, your confidence. One email and suddenly you're asking yourself, was I not good enough? At the risk of sounding like a motivational speaker, let me assure you, you are. But if you're going through a layoff, do not panic. Pivot. And keep these five tips in mind. The first step is to accept it, but don't resign to it. There's a huge difference between the two. Denial is comfortable, but it is also dangerous. You can't fight a storm you refuse to see. You can grieve the job. That's completely valid. Allow yourself time to process your emotions. But don't stay stuck in a negative loop. Easier said than done, I know. But try to stay positive. You are more than your job. The second thing, then, you need to do is upskill. Treat this with urgency. Remember, the companies doing the cuts are the same ones investing in AI tools, automation and digital transformation. That is your roadmap. Learn what they're buying into, whether it's prompt engineering, data analysis, cloud certifications or product thinking. Pick a lane and persist. In today's world, your degree, your degrees get you hired once. But your skills, that is what keeps you alive in the job market. Number three, then, rebuild your CV. Get your documents in order before the opportunity arrives. Most resumes are boring. They have long paragraphs, buzzwords, but zero impact. You don't want that for yourself. Keep your CV sharp and short. Lead with a one liner that tells the hiring manager who you are in under 10 seconds. Highlight impact, not duties. Because recruiters don't read resumes. They scan it for value. So make sure you stand out. The fourth tip is networking. I know this is where most people hesitate. You don't want to bother others. You're reluctant to intrude, perhaps. You don't want to be overbearing. But keep in mind that company HRs get hundreds of CVs and online applications. Online applications, in fact, are worse. Think of them as crowded highways and referrals as fast lanes. Let me give you the numbers here. Roughly 82% of job seekers apply for jobs via the Internet. How many of them land an interview? Just 2%. How are you going to make yourself visible? So reach out to old managers, former colleagues, mentors perhaps that you haven't spoken to in years. Most roles are filled before they're ever posted. Referrals are gold. Don't just spray applications into the void. Build real conversations. And finally, the most important tip of all, protect your mental health. A layoff can shake your identity to its core. You start waking up with anxiety. You scroll through LinkedIn. Maybe you feel behind. You start comparing yourself to somebody else's job highlight. Don't fall into that trap. Reach out to a friend. You need to have a daily routine. Eat healthy. Meditate. Exercise. We are, after all, what we feed our body and mind. So 40% caffeine and 60% existential dread, that's not going to help. The currency in the job market now is adaptability. People who use this moment to upskill, reconnect and rebuild, they don't just survive layoffs. They come back stronger. Every resume that exists today once belonged to someone who was starting over. Remember, success is not a checklist. Don't live a life or build a career that hinges on comparison. Make room for fear and failure. But don't make it your whole personality. Anything that is worthwhile in life won't come easy. So stay in the race because, as Guns N' Roses said in their famous song, nothing lasts forever.
